First, we load the test images and visualize their histograms. The CDF, then, is easy to calculate as cumulative sum of the histogram:

Of course, the images have extremely poor contrast. Typically, when discussing the anatomy histograms, photographers will refer to the “shadows, midtones, and highlights”. Next, we perform histogram equalization. This will help to improve the contrast and exposure simultaneously. In order to accomplish this, we essentially “stretch” the CDF so that it spans the diagonal.

 

  

The equalized histograms now span almost the entire range intensities.

One interesting detail to note is the difference between the first equalized histogram and the other two. The first image, as shown in its original histogram, exhibited data clipping. Even after the equalization step, this clipping is still evident because clipped exposure data cannot be recovered. Therefore, the spike on the right side of the first equalized histogram still dominates.

Another important note is the observed sparsity of these histograms. The original histograms were very low contrast and therefore exposure data occupied a small number of bins. Histogram equalization does not interpolate the exposure data between bins, so the processed images still occupy the same number of bins, just “spread out” more.

K essentially acts as a threshold parameter based on the gradient magnitude. The ratio between K and the gradient magnitude is intuitively important for both equations, and it can be observed from the images above that as K increases, stronger edges will begin to smooth over. For the highest K values, the image becomes blurry, and for low K values, there is no noticeable reduction in noise. The intermediate values (that is, 0.025-0.01, depending on the coefficient calculation and image) oftentimes provided the best balance for reducing noise without smoothing over edges.

Next, we explore the effects of changing the number of iterations (here, dt=0.01).

 

 

As each iteration applies the diffusion function once, more iterations intuitively result in more smoothing. Compared with K values, however, the limiting case as iterations increase tended to preserve edges better. For K = 0.05 in the CT scan (Image 2), for example the strong edges remain sharp while significantly blurring everything else, even as we applied 500 iterations. This process is very similar to numerically calculating the solution to the Laplace Heat Equation on a 2D surface, but the conduction coefficients are dependent on the gradient. So, it can be expected that the limiting case will result in convergence to some solution of the above differential equations.

 

(b) Comparison with Gaussian Smoothing

 

Next, we will consider the comparison of Anisotropic Diffusion with basic Gaussian blurring.

 

Compared with a simple Gaussian filter, it is evident that Anisotropic Diffusion possesses far superior abilities in edge preservation. Additionally, repeated applications of the diffusion process may not degrade strong edges, whereas with the Gaussian filter, edge degradation is guaranteed. This is because the Gaussian filter does not consider the magnitude of the gradient in its application, it is simply a Gaussian-weighted average of pixel values. Anisotropic Diffusion, in contrast, weights its filtering based on the magnitude of the gradient, allowing strong edges to be ignored in the blurring process.

 

(c) Limitations of Anisotropic Diffusion

 

Several limitations are inherent in Anisotropic Diffusion.

First, it only considers local information when identifying edges, ignoring the contextual patterns that humans use to distinguish meaningful edges from noise. Second, the choice of K is not arbitrary and heavily depends on the image we are processing. As shown above, a different K value worked best for image 1 than for image 2. As such, manual tuning is likely necessary in order to choose the parameters for Anisotropic Diffusion, which limits automated edge detection workflows. Finally, fine textures often have gradient magnitudes similar to noise, leading to unwanted smoothing of important textural details. For example, in the CT image, the structural details inside the brain region were oftentimes not preserved after many diffusion steps.

A potential alternative, from my own experience, would be the implementation of convolutional neural networks or other deep learning approaches. Convolutional neural networks trained on large sets of noisy data learn the kernels necessary to extract and preserve edge details, and convolutional autoencoders that learn to compress images can also be used to denoise them.
